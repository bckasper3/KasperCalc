Dynamic Viscosity of Propylene Glycol
What Is Dynamic Viscosity?

Dynamic (absolute) viscosity (μ) measures a fluid's resistance to shear flow. It is measured in mPa·s (millipascal-seconds), equivalent to cP (centipoise). Newton's law of viscosity is:

τ = μ × (du / dy)

where τ is shear stress and du/dy is the velocity gradient perpendicular to flow.

Why It Matters for Propylene Glycol Systems

Viscosity drives pressure drop, pump power requirements, and — critically — flow regime. The Reynolds number determines whether flow is laminar or turbulent:

Re = ρ × v × D / μ

At low temperatures, propylene glycol viscosity increases dramatically — especially at higher concentrations — pushing systems into laminar flow (Re < 2300) where heat transfer efficiency drops sharply. Pump curves must be re-evaluated when glycol is added to a system that was originally sized for water.

Note: Viscosity is highly temperature-dependent in propylene glycol solutions. Always check viscosity at the lowest expected operating temperature, not just at design conditions.
