Temperature Gradient Compensation Simulator
Which approaches benefit from active temperature compensation?
Approaches ①②④⑤ measure raw field components (Bz, |B|) that scale directly with Br(T). A temperature gradient shifts Br differently along the magnet length, biasing the null estimate — active compensation from your on-sensor temperature readings is essential for these.
Approach ③ (θ = atan2(Bx, Bz)) is intrinsically stable under uniform temperature because both components scale together, cancelling in the ratio. However a gradient still causes a small residual because the axial and radial field components sample different axial slices of the magnet at different temperatures. This simulation quantifies that residual for all five approaches and fits the degree-4 correction polynomial the MCU can apply using the measured ΔT from your sensor temperature readings.
Dimensional Tolerance Sensitivity Simulator
How does magnet manufacturing tolerance affect calibration accuracy?
The calibration map is built from the nominal magnet geometry. If the real magnet has a different OD, ID, or length — due to manufacturing tolerances — the null zones shift and the field shape changes, but the MCU still uses the nominal polynomial. This sweep quantifies the resulting gap error per approach across a range of dimensional deviations (e.g. ±4 mils on OD, ID, and L independently). It answers: which approach is most sensitive to tolerance stack-up, and how tight do your dimensions need to be to stay within your gap accuracy budget?
Note: the calibration map must be built first. Use the Δ OD / Δ ID / Δ L inputs in the MCU Accuracy Simulator above to simulate a single offset point; use this sweep to see the full sensitivity curve.

DOE assumptions and interpretation
The live magnet uses the sampled OD, ID, L, mean temperature, gradient, and thermal expansion. The temperature-correction factor is treated as available from ideal sensor temperature readings, so the residual gradient effect remains but uniform Br drift is compensated as in the current MCU simulator. IC placement errors are deliberately unmodeled by the nominal inversion coordinates to represent package/PCB tolerance or movement after calibration. The factor screen is a marginal randomized DOE score, not a full Sobol decomposition: interactions remain in the cases and a large score means that factor materially changes error over the entered range.
Q & A — Position from field readings

Q: If I accurately know the distance between my two sensors and I know both sensors' field readings, do I have enough information to determine my exact position?

Yes — in principle that is exactly enough information. You have one unknown (your axial midpoint position ymid) and two independent equations — one per sensor — both of which must be satisfied simultaneously given the fixed sensor separation ΔyAB:

B(ymid + ΔyAB/2) = Bytop
B(ymid − ΔyAB/2) = Bybot

Any ymid that satisfies both equations — given the known field profile B(y) of your magnet — is your position. Because you have two equations and only one unknown, the system is overdetermined: you can solve either equation independently, then use the other as a check. In a noise-free world this gives you a unique, exact answer.

Q: B(y) looks complicated globally — is there an ambiguity problem?

Monotonic means a function that moves in only one direction — always increasing, or always decreasing — as its input increases. A thermometer reading that always goes up as temperature rises is monotonic. If it went up, then down, then up again, it would not be monotonic, because the same output value could appear at multiple different inputs. For a monotonic function you can always work backwards uniquely: given the output, there is exactly one input that produced it.

B(y) is not monotonic globally. Look at the centerline chart above: the field rises near the pole faces, crosses zero at the null zones, reverses sign, and then decays far from the magnet. Over its full range, the same By value appears at multiple positions along the curve.

But that global picture is irrelevant to your application. Your sensors only move a few thousandths of an inch from their installed position. Over that tiny range, B(y) is smooth, well-behaved, and effectively monotonic — the curve does not turn around or cross zero within a span of a few thou. Within your operating window a single By reading maps to exactly one position. The global non-monotonicity is a concern for a system trying to locate itself anywhere along the full axis from scratch; it is not a concern for a system that is already installed near its operating point and tracking small excursions from there.

Q: So what actually limits my position accuracy?

Four real-world factors limit how well the math works in practice:

  1. Sensor separation uncertainty. ΔyAB is set by PCB manufacturing tolerance, not by a real-time measurement. Any error in ΔyAB propagates directly into the position estimate — it is the dominant error source at room temperature.
  2. Temperature drift of Br. Br changes with magnet temperature (typically −0.1 to −0.12 %/°C for NdFeB). This scales the entire field profile up or down, shifting the apparent position even when nothing has moved. The average By (shown in the readout panel) tracks this common-mode drift and can be used to compensate.
  3. Magnet geometry knowledge. The inversion B−1(By) depends on the field profile, which requires knowing L, OD, ID, and Br of your specific magnet. Tolerance on these parameters introduces a systematic offset in the position solution.
  4. Ferromagnetic debris. Debris near the magnet distorts the local field, shifting the apparent null positions and introducing an error that looks like a position change. This is the target phenomenon the sensor pair is designed to detect — so debris is a signal, not just noise.

Q: What does "calibration" do in this context?

Calibration freezes the sensor readings and gradient (k = dBy/dy) at a known reference state — typically with the assembly clean and at operating temperature. From that point on, the system tracks changes from the calibrated position rather than solving for absolute position from scratch. This sidesteps the ΔyAB and magnet-geometry uncertainties entirely: you no longer need to know where you are in absolute terms, only how far you have moved from where you were when things were good. Temperature drift appears as a common-mode shift in both sensors (trackable via By avg), while debris appears as a differential shift — the two effects are separable.

Q: Is absolute position or change-from-calibration more useful for debris detection?

Change-from-calibration is almost always more useful. Absolute position requires knowing Br, magnet geometry, and ΔyAB to high accuracy — none of which are typically known to the precision that debris detection demands. The differential ΔBy approach used in this prototype cancels common-mode effects (temperature, global Br drift) and isolates the asymmetric null shift that debris produces, all without needing any of those absolute quantities.

How the numerical engine works

The field map and centerline plot are computed from the magnetic scalar potential of the ring magnet. The magnet is treated as two surface charge distributions — one on each pole face — plus a volume charge term that appears when a temperature gradient is applied (because Br varies linearly through the thickness, making ∇·M ≠ 0 inside).

Each surface integral over the annular pole face is evaluated with 32-node Gauss-Legendre quadrature in the radial direction and a 32-point midpoint rule in the azimuthal direction. The midpoint rule is chosen deliberately for the azimuthal axis: because the integrand is periodic over [0, 2π], the midpoint rule is spectrally accurate and exactly symmetric about the magnet axis — Gauss-Legendre nodes are not mirror-symmetric, which would otherwise inject a spurious left/right field asymmetry. The volume term (temperature gradient only) adds a 24-node GL sweep in z.

Field components Bx and Bz are obtained by numerical differentiation of the potential (central-difference, h = 0.1 µm). The heatmap uses a faster 4×4 GL / 24-point midpoint kernel for speed; the centerline chart and self-tests use the full-accuracy 32×32 kernel.

The analytical (Bᵣ eqn) overlay on the centerline chart uses the closed-form ring-magnet formula — identical to the one shown above — with a uniform Bᵣ equal to the average remanence across both pole faces. The two curves should coincide closely at uniform temperature; any gap at a large temperature differential reflects the axial Bᵣ gradient that the closed-form formula cannot capture.
